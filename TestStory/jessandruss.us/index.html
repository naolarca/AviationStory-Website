The Story Of Aviation














  • Pre 347 BC

    The Greek Myth Of Icarus
    Icarus and his father Daedalus were imprisoned on the top of the tallest tower in the kingdom of Crete. Daedalus crafted two pairs of giant wings with feather and wax to escape. When Icarus and Daedalus started to fly, Icarus flew too close to the sun. Icarus’ wings melted and he fell to his death, causing Daedalus to live with regret.

  • Pre 347 BC

    Shahnameh by Ferdowsi
    Shay Kay Kavus of Greater Iran was flying with a craft that had four chained eagles lifting the craft into the sky. Shah Kay Kavus used the craft when travelling to China.


T he original myths spoke of ancient legends of human flight that captivated the imagination of large audiences. Two stories from Greek and Persian Mythologies were particularly powerful in captivating mankind’s imagination on the possibilities of flight.


  • Around 300 BC

    Kites
    The kite may have been the first form of manmade aircraft. It was invented in China possibly by Mozi and Lu Ban.

  • Around 300 BC

    Rotor wings
    The use of a rotor for vertical flight has existed in the form of bamboocopter, an ancient Chinese toy.

The Greek Myth Of Icarus

  • Around 300 BC

    Steam-Powered Wooden Pigeon
    Ancient Greek Mathematician Archytas of Tarentum reportedly created a steam-powered pigeon model. This wooden pigeon was launched projectile style into the sky and was a major breakthrough in artificially replicating the art of flight.

  • 3rd century BC

    Sky Lanterns
    From ancient times the Chinese have understood that hot air rises and have applied the principle to a type of small hot air balloon called a sky lantern.

Sky Lanterns were used in a cultural manner in Chinese society, they were used as a means of communication in the military. However, with the passing of time, it shifted to symbolizing a family reunion.

  • 800 - 1000

    Winged-Flight Attempts
    Several winged-flight attempts were made by Andalusian inventor Abbas ibn Firnas and English Benedictine monk Eilmer of Malmesbury. They tried covering themselves with feathers and wings, and flung themself down into the air. These attempts did not ultimately succeed, but they encouraged other scientists and mathematicians to explore the possibilities of air travel.

  • 1505-06

    Leonardo da Vinci
    Italian artist and inventor Leonardo da Vinci wrote a book called Codex on the Flight of Birds containing thousands of notes and hundreds of sketches on the nature of flight and aerodynamic principles that will influence the development of aviation and manmade aircraft.

Leonardo Da Vinci sketches

  • 1709

    The Pasarolla Airship
    Brazilian-Portuguese priest Bartolomeu de Gusmao presented King Joao V of Portugal with a petition, requesting the King’s favor in constructing an airship called Pasarolla. The Pasarolla airship never flew, but the design was an inspiration to future aircraft designers.

  • December 4th, 1782

    The Chamber Lift-Off Experiment
    Joseph-Michel Montgolfier and Jacques-Etienne Montgolfier built a box-like chamber out of thin wood, covering the sides and top with taffeta cloth. When they lit a controlled fire from under the box, the contraption rose into the air. The lift-off from the fire was so powerful, that they ended up losing control of their creation.

According to original sources, the basic idea behind the hot air balloon came from Joseph-Michel Montgolfier observing laundry over a fire. As the clothed dried, the pockets incidentally billowed upwards.

  • June 4th, 1783

    The first Hot Air Balloon Launch The Montgolfier Brothers publicly flew their balloon at Annona, France in front of a crowd of people. The balloon had an airtime of approximately 10 minutes. The balloon reached an estimate altitude that was between 1,600m and 2,000m Jacques-Etienne Montgolfier travelled to Paris to patent the invention of flight.

  • September 19th, 1783

    The Aerostat Réveillon Launch
    Jacques-Etienne Montgolfier and wallpaper manufacturer Jean-Baptiste Réveillon launched the Aerostat Réveillon at the Royal Palace in Versailles, France. It was the first balloon to have livestock riding as passengers: a sheep, a cockerel, and a duck. After a flight that lasted approximately 8 minutes, the craft safely landed 2 miles away from the palace, with all three animals surviving unharmed.

The Aerostat Reveillon Launch

  • November 21st, 1783

    The final hot air balloon launches
    Jacques-Etienne Montgolfier was the first human to travel in the balloon, followed by chemist Jean-Francois Pilatre De Rozier. After that, both Jean-Francois Pilatre de Rozier and army officer Marquis d’Arlandes conducted the first free-flight by humans, travelling 5 miles above the city of Paris. After a 25 minutes ride, the balloon landed and was lauded with praise.

  • August 27th, 1783

    The First Hydrogen Balloon Test
    Anne-Jean Robert and Nicholas-Louis Robert built the world’s first hydrogen balloon with French Professor Jacques Charles. Professor Jacques Charles conceived that hydrogen would an ideal power source due to its chemical properties.



The first hot air balloon consisted of a dome and three lateral bands, held together by 1800 buttons. A fishing net of cord covered the outside of the balloon to give it stability.

The first Hydrogen Balloon

  • December 1st, 1783

    The first hydrogen balloon manned flight
    The Robert Brothers’ hydrogen balloon had a barometer and a thermometer, which made it the first balloon to record meteorological measurements of the Earth’s atmosphere during its flight. It is reported that 400,000 people gathered at the Imperial Palace in Paris to watch the launch. Among the viewers were renowned American scientist Benjamin Franklin and iconic French inventor Joseph-Michel Montgolfier.

  • Following years

    The problem of altitude sickness
    On a solo flight of the hydrogen balloon, Professor Jacques Charles noted his run-in with altitude sickness and hearing pain, after reaching an altitude of around 9,800 feet. This was a problem that scientists and inventors would try to fix when designing future aircraft models.



They discovered the feeling of hypoxia with high altitude: "Towards 7500 meters, the numbness one experiences is extraordinary…. One does not suffer at all; on the contrary. One experiences inner joy, as if it were an effect of the inundating flood of light. One becomes indifferent…. Soon I wanted to seize the oxygen tube, but could not raise my arm…. Suddenly I closed my eyes and fell inert, entirely losing consciousness." ~ Gaston Tissandier

Jacques Charles Professor



  • 1809-1820

    Sir George Cayley introduces aerodynamics
    English philosopher George Cayley published On Aerial Navigation, a radical series of papers credited with introducing the world to the study of aerodynamics. Cayley found the secrets of lift in the shape of a bird’s wing, surmising that an arched, or cambered, wing would produce greater lift than a flat wing because of lower pressure on top of the curved surface.




  • In the 1870s

    Otto Lilienthal discoveries
    Otto Lilienthal, a German mechanical engineer, undertook the most important studies of wing design since the time of Cayley. Having published the results of his research, Lilienthal designed, built and flew a series of monoplane and biplane gliders. He started the origins of hang gliding.





From all the foregoing results it appears obvious that in order to discover the principles which facilitate flight, and to eventually enable man to fly, we must take the bird for our model.

George Cayley's Invention

    The Giffard Dirigible Launch
    Henri Giffard made the first steam-engine powered flight, travelling from Paris to Elancourt. The Giffard Dirigible was steerable, which allowed Henri Giffard to guide the direction of his airship.   • 1876

    The internal combustion engine changes everything
    German engineer Nikolaus Otto devised a lighter, more efficient, gas-powered combustion engine, providing an alternative to the previously universal steam-powered engine.






Ballooning experienced a surge of growth in the late 19th century. Balloons were used for entertainment, research, and warfare. Balloons were used by the Union Army in reconnaissance missions during U.S. Civil War.

  • 1884

    La France Dirigble
    Charles Renard and Arthur Krebs launched their first fully controllable free-flight engine-powered dirigible named La France. La France dirigible utilized 8 ½ horsepower electric-motor, covered 8 kilometers in its first launch, and was notable for accomplishing the first successful round-trip flight.

  • In the late 1800s

    The Santos-Dumont No.6 Dirigible Model
    Brazilian Aviator Alberto Santos-Dumont became a national hero in Brazil for pushing the limits of aviation with his newest dirigible design. The Santos Dumont No.6 dirigible was powered by a balloon and a 12 horsepower internal combustion engine, which drove the airship propellor.





From that day on, I no longer had the slightest doubt about the success of my invention. I recognized that I would, for life, be dedicated to aircraft construction." ~ Alberto Santos-Dumont

Alberto Santos-Dumont invented the Santos-Dumont No.20 Demoiselle airship, which was a major breakthrough for ultralight aircraft development.

  • Late 19th century

    The Success Of The Blimp The blimp model was notable for relying upon the use of lifting gases like helium, rather than flammable gases like hydrogen and for not having an internal structural framework, which a typical rigid airship model would possess.

  • Start of the 20th century

    Rigid Airship Success The rigid airship is notable for maintaining its shape without the use of pressure from lifting gas and utilizing an internal framework that fully supports the envelope of the craft.





Dirigible models were typically very frail and they were notorious for achieving lackluster airtime. The solution to these problems came in the form of non-rigid airships, known as blimps.

Ferdinand von Zeppelin focused on increasing cargo carrying-capacity, which would be key for mounting military grade weapons on top of airships.

  • December 17th, 1903

    The Wright Flyer/Launch
    The original Wright Flyer was inspired by the 1902 Wright Glider and was built entirely out of Spruce wood, was powered by 12 horsepower gasoline engine and had two counter rotating pusher propellers that stopped torque from occurring The propellers were powered through a sprocket chain drive. The original Wright Flyer wings were symmetrical by design to stabilize the plane. The first flight of the Wright Flyer I model conducted by Orville Wright, was captured in a famous photograph by John T. Daniels and it travelled a total distance of 120 feet.

  • December 17th, 1903

    Subsequent Flights By The Wright Flyer I
    The next flight of the Wright Flyer I conducted on the same day by Wilbur Wright, travelled a total distance of 175 feet The final flight of the Wright Flyer I conducted on the same day by Orville Wright, travelled a total distance of 200 feet.








The Wright Flyer was the most crucial design to influence modern day military and commercial aviation. It was designed by the Wright Brothers: Orville Wright and Wilbur Wright. The Wright Flyer was the first piloted engine powered airplane.

Orville and Wilbur Wright are credited with inventing the airplane. They were the first to make a successful human flight with a craft that was powered by an engine and was heavier than air. This was quite a milestone and impacted transportation throughout the world.

The overall purpose of the Autogyro was to create a flight machine that could successfully achieve low-speed flight.
